اِقْرَأْ

The House of Wisdom

A reading companion to the scientists of the Islamic Golden Age — the scholars who, between the 8th and 15th centuries, took the inheritance of Greece, Persia, and India and built from it algebra, experimental optics, clinical medicine, and observatory astronomy.

8th–15th centuries Baghdad · Cairo · Córdoba · Samarkand Iqraʾ — “Read,” the first revealed word (Q 96:1)

The astrolabe: “a mathematical jewel” — computer, star map, and timepiece of the medieval world.

I · Introduction

A civilisation that read

For roughly seven centuries, the language of science was Arabic — written by Muslims, Christians, Jews, and Sabians, from Córdoba to Samarkand, under the patronage of caliphs, viziers, and sultans.

The story usually begins in Baghdad. Founded in 762 by the Abbasid caliph al-Mansur as a planned round city, it became within two generations the largest city in the world outside China — and the centre of the most ambitious knowledge-transfer project antiquity’s heirs ever attempted. From roughly the mid-8th to the 10th century, nearly the entire surviving corpus of Greek secular learning — Aristotle, Euclid, Ptolemy, Galen, Archimedes — was translated into Arabic, alongside Persian administrative and astronomical works and Indian mathematical texts. The historian Dimitri Gutas has shown that this was no marginal scribal hobby: it was a society-wide enterprise, sustained for over two centuries by the Abbasid elite, with translators commanding enormous salaries.1

The emblem of the movement is the Bayt al-Ḥikma بيت الحكمة — the “House of Wisdom” — a palace library and academy that flourished under the caliph al-Maʾmūn (r. 813–833). Al-Maʾmūn did not merely collect books; he commissioned new science. His astronomers re-measured the obliquity of the ecliptic, compiled the “Verified Tables” (al-Zīj al-Mumtaḥan), and famously measured the length of a degree of latitude on the plain of Sinjar to estimate the circumference of the Earth.1, 17 The greatest of the translators, the Nestorian Christian physician Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq (808–873), rendered virtually the whole of Galen into Syriac and Arabic with a philologist’s rigour — collating manuscripts, comparing readings — that anticipates modern critical editing.1, 10

Two material facts made all of this scale. First, paper: the craft reached the Islamic world through Central Asia after the battle of Talas (751), and paper mills in Baghdad made books cheap enough for a genuine book trade — Ibn al-Nadīm’s Fihrist (987), a bookseller’s catalogue, lists thousands of titles. Second, a single scientific lingua franca: a student in Córdoba and an astronomer in Samarkand read and wrote the same Arabic, so results travelled.2, 17

Why does the era matter? Not because “the Arabs preserved Greek science for Europe” — a formula historians of science now regard as both condescending and false. As George Saliba and others have documented, scholars writing in Arabic transformed what they received: they invented algebra as a discipline, founded experimental optics, described pulmonary circulation, built the first research observatories, and developed planetary models later found, virtually line for line, in Copernicus.2, 9 This page is a guided shelf: each section gives the context, the key figures, and the sources to read next.

II · Timeline

Seven centuries in five movements

Select any era to expand it. Dates are CE.

750 – 809 The Abbasid dawn expand ✦

The Abbasid revolution (750) shifts the caliphate’s centre of gravity east, toward Persia’s courtly and scholarly traditions. Baghdad is founded in 762; within decades Indian astronomical texts (the Sindhind tradition, carrying Hindu numerals and sine tables) are translated at the caliph al-Mansur’s request. Papermaking arrives via Central Asia after the battle of Talas (751). The corpus attributed to the alchemist Jābir ibn Ḥayyān begins to take shape. Under Hārūn al-Rashīd (r. 786–809), the palace library that would become the House of Wisdom is already collecting Greek and Persian manuscripts.1, 17

813 – 873 Al-Maʾmūn and the translation movement’s peak expand ✦

Al-Maʾmūn (r. 813–833) turns patronage into policy: embassies seek Greek manuscripts, observatories operate at Shammāsiyya (Baghdad) and Mount Qāsiyūn (Damascus) around 828–832, and a degree of latitude is measured to gauge the Earth. In this single lifetime work: al-Khwārizmī (algebra, the Hindu numerals), al-Kindī (“Philosopher of the Arabs”), the Banū Mūsā brothers (geometry, mechanics), and Ḥunayn ibn Isḥāq (the Galenic corpus). Thābit ibn Qurra (836–901), a Sabian from Harran, arrives soon after — number theory, statics, and revisions of Euclid and Ptolemy.1, 4

900 – 1050 The age of the giants expand ✦

Political fragmentation multiplies courts — and therefore patrons. Al-Battānī refines solar theory at Raqqa; al-Rāzī directs hospitals in Rayy and Baghdad; al-Uqlīdisī (Damascus, 952) writes the earliest surviving treatment of decimal fractions; Abū al-Wafāʾ advances spherical trigonometry in Buyid Baghdad. Around the year 1000, three careers overlap that would each anchor a century: Ibn Sīnā in Persia, Ibn al-Haytham in Cairo, and al-Bīrūnī in Ghazna — the last measuring the Earth’s radius from a mountaintop in Punjab to within about 1% of the modern value. Córdoba’s caliphal library and Cairo’s Dār al-ʿIlm (1004) rival Baghdad.4, 5, 17

1050 – 1250 Consolidation, critique, and transmission expand ✦

The madrasa spreads as an institution of learning. ʿUmar al-Khayyām classifies cubic equations and leads the Jalālī calendar reform (1079) — more accurate than the Gregorian calendar that followed five centuries later. Al-Ghazālī’s Incoherence of the Philosophers (c. 1095) and Ibn Rushd’s reply frame Islam’s great debate on reason and causation. Al-Jazarī (d. 1206) documents crankshafts, camshafts, and programmable automata. Meanwhile in Toledo, Gerard of Cremona (d. 1187) translates over seventy Arabic works into Latin — the Almagest, Avicenna’s Canon, al-Khwārizmī’s algebra — feeding the new European universities.14, 15

1258 – 1450 After Baghdad: Maragheh, Damascus, Samarkand expand ✦

The Mongol sack of Baghdad (1258) ends the Abbasid caliphate — yet the following two centuries produce some of the era’s most sophisticated science, a fact that by itself refutes the old “decline after al-Ghazālī” narrative.2 Hülegü founds the Maragheh observatory (1259) under Naṣīr al-Dīn al-Ṭūsī, whose “Ṭūsī couple” resolves a flaw in Ptolemy’s models. Ibn al-Nafīs describes pulmonary circulation in Cairo (c. 1242). Ibn al-Shāṭir perfects non-Ptolemaic planetary models in Damascus (d. 1375). At Samarkand, Ulugh Beg’s observatory (c. 1420) and al-Kāshī’s computational genius produce the finest star catalogue and trigonometric tables before Tycho Brahe.7, 8, 9

III · Mathematics

Al-jabr: the science of restoring

Two of the most common words in modern mathematics — algebra and algorithm — are, respectively, an Arabic noun and a Latinised Arabic surname.

Mathematicians writing in Arabic fused three inheritances: Greek geometry and proof, Indian numerals and computation, and Babylonian-Persian practical arithmetic. Out of that fusion came genuinely new departments of mathematics. Algebra became a discipline with its own name and methods. Trigonometry was systematised as a subject independent of astronomy — all six modern trigonometric functions were defined and tabulated by Arabic authors, with Abū al-Wafāʾ (940–998) proving the angle-addition identities on the sphere.4 Decimal fractions appear in al-Uqlīdisī’s Damascus arithmetic of 952; Thābit ibn Qurra gave a rule generating amicable numbers; and combinatorics, number theory, and geometric constructions all advanced.4, 5

Muḥammad ibn Mūsā al-Khwārizmī

Latin: Algoritmi c. 780 – c. 850 · Baghdad

The astronomer of the House of Wisdom whose two short books named both algebra and the algorithm.

Life

Of Khwarazmian (Central Asian) origin, al-Khwārizmī worked in Baghdad under the caliph al-Maʾmūn as an astronomer and head of the House of Wisdom’s library. He produced a zīj (astronomical handbook), a world geography correcting Ptolemy, and the two mathematical works that made his name immortal.4, 18

Contributions

  • Al-Kitāb al-mukhtaṣar fī ḥisāb al-jabr wa-l-muqābala (“The Compendious Book on Calculation by Restoration and Balancing,” c. 820) gives the first systematic treatment of solving linear and quadratic equations — classified into standard types, solved by general procedures, and justified with geometric proofs. The operation al-jabr (“restoring” a subtracted quantity to the other side) gave algebra its name.4
  • His treatise on reckoning with Hindu numerals — surviving only in Latin as Dixit Algorizmi (“Thus spoke al-Khwārizmī”) — introduced decimal place-value arithmetic, including zero, to the Arabic world and thence to Europe. Medieval Latin writers called any decimal procedure an algorismus: our “algorithm.”4, 3

Why it mattered

Al-Khwārizmī deliberately wrote for practical users — merchants, surveyors, judges dividing inheritances — and in doing so abstracted equation-solving away from any particular problem. That abstraction, the idea of the equation as an object in its own right, is the founding move of algebra. Translated by Robert of Chester (1145) and Gerard of Cremona, the Algebra was the seed text of European mathematics for four centuries.4, 15

ʿUmar al-Khayyām

Omar Khayyam 1048 – 1131 · Nishapur, Isfahan

Poet of the Rubāʿiyāt — and the mathematician who classified and solved cubic equations with conic sections.

Contributions

  • His Treatise on Demonstration of Problems of Algebra (c. 1070) gives the first complete classification of cubic equations (fourteen types) and solves each geometrically as the intersection of two conic sections — the deepest work on cubics before the Italian algebraists of the 1500s.4
  • Led the astronomers of Malik-Shāh’s Isfahan observatory in the Jalālī calendar reform (1079), whose intercalation scheme yields a year accurate to about one day in 5,000 years — better than the Gregorian calendar.5, 17
  • In his commentary on Euclid he analysed the parallel postulate through what is now called the Khayyām–Saccheri quadrilateral, a step on the long road to non-Euclidean geometry.4

Why it mattered

Khayyām explicitly recognised that cubics might need means beyond ruler and compass — a clear-eyed statement of the limits of classical construction, and an early instance of mathematics reflecting on its own methods.

Ghiyāth al-Dīn al-Kāshī

al-Kashi c. 1380 – 1429 · Kashan, Samarkand

The computational virtuoso of Ulugh Beg’s Samarkand — π to sixteen decimal places, by hand, in 1424.

Contributions

  • In the Treatise on the Circumference (1424) he computed 2π to nine sexagesimal places — sixteen decimal digits — using polygons of over 800 million sides, with a careful error analysis. The record stood for nearly two centuries.4, 5
  • His Miftāḥ al-Ḥisāb (“Key to Arithmetic,” 1427) is a comprehensive manual of decimal fractions and computation for astronomers, architects, and merchants.
  • Devised a rapidly converging fixed-point iteration to compute sin 1° to extraordinary precision — an algorithm in the fully modern sense.4

Why it mattered

Al-Kāshī shows the tradition at full maturity: not preserving old results but pushing numerical method, error control, and systematic computation to limits Europe would not match until Viète and Ludolph van Ceulen.

IV · Astronomy

Reading the sky, correcting Ptolemy

Islamic ritual gave astronomy daily work — prayer times, the qibla, the lunar calendar — but the science soon outgrew its chores and began interrogating Ptolemy himself.

The characteristic product was the zīj: a handbook of astronomical tables with instructions, of which more than two hundred were composed — a continuous, self-correcting observational tradition unmatched anywhere before early-modern Europe.6 Its characteristic institution was the observatory as a funded research organisation with large instruments, staff, and a scientific director — an Islamic invention, culminating at Maragheh and Samarkand.2, 7 And its characteristic instrument was the astrolabe, the analogue computer of the age, on which David King’s studies remain the standard reference.7

“It is He who made the sun a radiance and the moon a light, and ordained for it phases, that you might know the number of years and the reckoning of time.”

Qurʾan 10:5 — a verse the zīj tradition took as a mandate

Al-Battānī

Latin: Albategnius c. 858 – 929 · Raqqa

The observer Copernicus cited by name — measurer of the solar year to within about two minutes.

Contributions

  • Nearly forty years of observations at Raqqa yielded the al-Zīj al-Ṣābiʾ: a solar year of 365 days, 5 hours, 46 minutes, 24 seconds (about 2½ minutes short of the modern value), an improved rate of precession, and the discovery that the solar apogee moves — i.e., Ptolemy’s solar model was not fixed for all time.6, 18
  • Replaced Greek chords with sines and tangents in his computations, helping to establish trigonometry’s modern functions.4

Why it mattered

Translated into Latin in the 12th century, his zīj became a staple of European astronomy; Copernicus cites “Albategnius” repeatedly in De revolutionibus. He demonstrated that sustained, precise observation could overturn received parameters — the empirical temper at its best.19

Ibn al-Shāṭir

 1304 – 1375 · Damascus

The mosque timekeeper whose planetary models reappear, mathematically identical, in Copernicus.

Contributions

  • As chief muwaqqit (timekeeper) of the Umayyad Mosque, he constructed a magnificent sundial and wrote on instruments — practical astronomy of the highest order.7
  • In The Final Quest Concerning the Rectification of Principles he rebuilt Ptolemy’s planetary models using only uniform circular motions, eliminating the equant and the eccentric — the culmination of the “Maragheh school” program begun with al-Ṭūsī’s couple.8, 9

Why it mattered

In 1957, E. S. Kennedy and Victor Roberts showed that Ibn al-Shāṭir’s lunar model is mathematically identical to the one Copernicus published in 1543, and his Mercury model likewise corresponds.9, 19 Whether Copernicus saw these results through Byzantine intermediaries or rediscovered them is still debated — but the geometry itself was worked out in 14th-century Damascus, in a geocentric framework, for reasons of physical and mathematical consistency.2

The great observatories: Maragheh & Samarkand

 1259 · 1420 — the research institute is born

State-funded campuses with giant instruments, libraries, staff astronomers — and results that outlived their patrons.

Maragheh (founded 1259, Azerbaijan, Iran)

Endowed by the Mongol ruler Hülegü and directed by Naṣīr al-Dīn al-Ṭūsī (1201–1274), Maragheh housed a library, a workshop for instruments, and an international staff. Its scientific signature is the Ṭūsī couple — two circles rolling one inside the other to generate straight-line motion from pure rotations — published in al-Ṭūsī’s Tadhkira to repair Ptolemy’s violation of uniform circular motion. The same device later appears in De revolutionibus.8, 19 The observatory’s collaborative model — and its zīj, the Zīj-i Īlkhānī — set the pattern for every successor.

Samarkand (founded c. 1420, Uzbekistan)

Built by the Timurid prince — and serious mathematician — Ulugh Beg (1394–1449), with a meridian sextant of roughly 40-metre radius sunk into a hillside. Its Zīj-i Sulṭānī (c. 1437) contains a catalogue of about 1,018 stars measured afresh — essentially the first comprehensive new star catalogue since Ptolemy — plus sine tables accurate to many sexagesimal places, computed under al-Kāshī and Qāḍī-zāde al-Rūmī.6, 7

V · Medicine

The Canon and the bimaristan

Medicine was where Islamic science touched the most lives — and where its books ruled European curricula the longest.

The institutional achievement was the bimaristan, the hospital as a charitable, teaching, and research institution. Ahmad ibn Ṭūlūn’s hospital in Cairo (872) offered free care with separate wards; Baghdad’s ʿAḍudī hospital (981) reportedly opened with two dozen physicians on staff, including specialists. Funded by perpetual religious endowments (waqf), bimaristans treated patients regardless of faith or means, kept teaching rounds, and in some cases wards for the mentally ill — an ethic of institutional care that Pormann and Savage-Smith’s standard survey documents in detail.10 In al-Andalus, al-Zahrāwī (Latin Abulcasis, 936–1013) devoted a volume of his medical encyclopaedia al-Taṣrīf to surgery, illustrating some two hundred instruments; it served as Europe’s surgical manual into the Renaissance.10, 18

Abū Bakr al-Rāzī

Latin: Rhazes c. 854 – 925 · Rayy, Baghdad

The clinician’s clinician: first to tell smallpox from measles, and a fearless critic — even of Galen.

Contributions

  • His treatise On Smallpox and Measles gives the first clear clinical differentiation of the two diseases, based on bedside observation; it was reprinted in Europe dozens of times into the 19th century.10
  • The posthumous Kitāb al-Ḥāwī (Latin Liber Continens), assembled from his working files, records symptoms, treatments, and case notes with unusual candour about failures — an early culture of the case report.10, 18
  • Wrote Doubts about Galen, testing inherited doctrine against observation — and, as a chemist, brought laboratory method to pharmacy.

Why it mattered

Al-Rāzī directed hospitals in Rayy and Baghdad and put experience above authority in print. Alongside Ibn Sīnā’s Canon, the Continens was among the most consulted medical books of medieval Europe.10, 11

Ibn Sīnā

Latin: Avicenna 980 – 1037 · Bukhara, Hamadan

Philosopher-physician whose Canon of Medicine was a university textbook — in Europe — for six hundred years.

Contributions

  • Al-Qānūn fī al-Ṭibb (completed 1025) organises the whole of Greco-Arabic medicine into a single systematic structure: principles, materia medica (about 760 drugs), diseases head-to-toe, and pharmacology — with attention to contagion, quarantine, and clinical testing of remedies.10, 11
  • His philosophical summa, Kitāb al-Shifāʾ (“The Book of Healing”), spans logic, natural science, mathematics, and metaphysics; Latin scholastics knew him simply as the great interpreter of Aristotle before Averroes.

Why it mattered

Translated by Gerard of Cremona, the Canon anchored the curricula of Montpellier, Bologna, and Padua; Nancy Siraisi has traced its printings and commentaries in Italy well into the 17th century — arguably the most influential single medical text ever written.11, 15

Ibn al-Nafīs

 1213 – 1288 · Damascus, Cairo

Described the pulmonary circulation of the blood — three centuries before European anatomists.

Contributions

In his Commentary on Anatomy in Avicenna’s Canon (c. 1242), Ibn al-Nafīs rejected Galen’s claim that blood passes through invisible pores in the heart’s septum. He reasoned that the septum is solid, and that blood must travel from the right ventricle through the lungs — where it mixes with air — before returning to the left ventricle: the pulmonary (lesser) circulation, stated clearly and argued anatomically.10

Why it mattered

Michael Servetus (1553) and Realdo Colombo (1559) published the same conclusion three centuries later; the Arabic priority was only rediscovered in 1924, in a Berlin manuscript, by the Egyptian physician Muhyī al-Dīn al-Tatāwī. Whether the European accounts drew indirectly on Ibn al-Nafīs (a Latin translation of some of his work existed) remains an open question — but the episode is a standing caution about how histories of “firsts” get written.10, 16

VI · Physics & Optics

The first experimentalist

If a single book marks the beginning of experimental physics, a strong case can be made for the Book of Optics, written in Cairo around a thousand years ago.

Ibn al-Haytham

Latin: Alhazen / Alhacen c. 965 – c. 1040 · Basra, Cairo

Settled a seven-century dispute about vision — by experiment — and wrote the method down.

Life

Born in Basra, Ibn al-Haytham came to Cairo under the Fatimid caliph al-Ḥākim — by one famous account after rashly proposing to regulate the Nile’s floods, then feigning madness when the project proved impossible. During years of quiet disgrace he wrote his masterpiece, the seven-book Kitāb al-Manāẓir (Book of Optics, composed c. 1011–1021).12, 18

Contributions

  • Vision solved. Greek theory was split between “extramission” (the eye emits rays) and “intromission” (the eye receives them). Ibn al-Haytham demolished extramission with arguments and experiments — afterimages, pain from staring at the sun — and built a mathematically rigorous intromission theory: light travels in straight lines from every point of an object into the eye.12, 3
  • The camera obscura (al-bayt al-muẓlim, “the dark room”) analysed quantitatively: image formation through small apertures, used to study eclipses — the ancestor of every camera.12
  • Method. Controlled, repeatable experiments with apparatus (dark chambers, tubes, mirrors) combined with geometric proof; he writes explicitly that the inquirer into the sciences must be “an adversary of all he reads,” submitting every claim to demonstration and test.12
  • “Alhazen’s problem”: given a light source and a spherical mirror, find the reflection point — a quartic problem he solved geometrically; a full algebraic solution waited until the 20th century. He also weighed in on refraction, atmospheric optics, and the perceived enlargement of the moon at the horizon as a psychological effect.3, 12

Why it mattered

Translated into Latin around 1200 as De aspectibus and printed by Friedrich Risner in 1572 as the Opticae thesaurus, the book shaped Roger Bacon, Witelo, and Pecham, and stands directly behind Kepler’s theory of the retinal image (1604). David Lindberg’s history of optics treats him as the pivotal figure between antiquity and Kepler; the historian of science A. I. Sabra, his editor and translator, called the Optics a work that changed the very standard of what counts as proof in natural science.3, 12

Beyond optics, Arabic natural philosophy probed motion and weight: Ibn Sīnā’s concept of mayl (inclination) and Abū al-Barakāt al-Baghdādī’s critique of Aristotelian dynamics fed the medieval impetus theory that preceded inertia; al-Bīrūnī and al-Khāzinī measured specific gravities of metals and gems with hydrostatic balances to remarkable precision.5, 16

VII · Chemistry

Al-kīmiyāʾ: the workshop tradition

Chemistry’s very name — like alkali, alcohol, alembic, and elixir — is Arabic. So is its founding habit: judging theory by what happens over the fire.

Jābir ibn Ḥayyān

Latin: Geber fl. 8th century (corpus 8th–10th c.) · Kufa

The name over the door of Arabic alchemy — and a corpus that turned mystical art toward laboratory practice.

The corpus

Tradition makes Jābir a student of the Shīʿī imam Jaʿfar al-Ṣādiq in 8th-century Kufa; modern scholarship (beginning with Paul Kraus) regards the enormous Jābirian corpus — nearly three thousand titles are attributed — as the product of a school writing into the 10th century. Either way, the corpus defined Arabic alchemy.13, 18

Contributions

  • Systematic descriptions of laboratory operations — distillation, sublimation, crystallisation, calcination — and of apparatus including the alembic (al-inbīq), giving alchemy a reproducible technical vocabulary.13
  • The sulphur–mercury theory of metals: all metals as combinations of two principles differing in purity and proportion — the reigning theory of matter’s composition until the 18th century.13
  • An insistence, quoted across the corpus, that the worker must perform experiments — knowledge in this art belongs to those who labour at the bench, not those who merely read.

Why it mattered

Latin Europe knew “Geber” as the supreme chemical authority; the 13th-century Summa perfectionis, written under his name (probably by Paul of Taranto), became the West’s standard chemistry textbook. Lawrence Principe’s history places the Arabic workshop tradition squarely at the root of experimental chemistry.13

The tradition’s second pillar is al-Rāzī — the same physician of Section V — whose Book of Secrets (Kitāb al-Asrār) drops mystical allegory almost entirely: it inventories substances in a rational classification (mineral, vegetable, animal; spirits, metals, stones, vitriols, boraxes, salts), lists apparatus piece by piece, and gives recipes precise enough to follow. Distillation technology matured in this milieu — including the distillation of wine noted by later authors, from which Europe would take both the technique and the word alcohol (الكحل, al-kuḥl).13, 16

VIII · Philosophy of Knowledge

Faith, reason, and the framing of science

The Qurʾan repeatedly commands its readers to observe and reflect — “Do they not look at the camels, how they are created; and the sky, how it is raised?” (Q 88:17–18) — and the civilisation took the command seriously.

Science in Islam did not grow in spite of religion in some neutral secular space; it grew inside a religious culture that prized knowledge (ʿilm) as a virtue, that needed astronomy and arithmetic for its rituals and law, and whose jurists developed — in ijtihād, disciplined independent reasoning — a methodological self-confidence that carried over into natural inquiry. Verses invoking the signs (āyāt) in creation “for a people who reflect” (e.g. Q 3:190–191) supplied a standing theological warrant for studying nature.16, 17 The philosophical tradition (falsafa) begun by al-Kindī (c. 801–873) and systematised by al-Fārābī (c. 872–950) argued explicitly that demonstrative reason and revelation, rightly understood, cannot ultimately conflict.

Al-Ghazālī

Latin: Algazel 1058 – 1111 · Tus, Baghdad

The theologian who audited philosophy — and whose alleged “killing of science” is a myth worth examining.

The argument

In Tahāfut al-Falāsifa (The Incoherence of the Philosophers, c. 1095), al-Ghazālī attacked twenty theses of the Aristotelian philosophers — above all the world’s eternity and the denial of bodily resurrection — and, famously, the claim that fire necessarily burns cotton. Causal connection, he argued, is not logically necessary; God’s custom (ʿāda) sustains the regularities we observe.14

The reassessment

The old story — Ghazālī discredits philosophy, Islamic science dies — fails on the evidence: he explicitly defends the certainty of mathematics and logic, warns believers not to deny demonstrated astronomy (such as the geometry of eclipses) in religion’s name, and the two centuries after him produced Maragheh, Ibn al-Nafīs, and Ibn al-Shāṭir. Frank Griffel’s study presents him as reconciling Avicennan science with theology rather than abolishing it; Saliba makes the institutional case against the decline myth.2, 14

Ibn Rushd

Latin: Averroes 1126 – 1198 · Córdoba, Marrakesh

Judge, physician, and “The Commentator” — Europe’s teacher of Aristotle, and al-Ghazālī’s great respondent.

The reply

A chief judge (qāḍī) of Córdoba and court physician, Ibn Rushd answered al-Ghazālī point by point in Tahāfut al-Tahāfut (The Incoherence of the Incoherence) and argued in the Decisive Treatise that the sharīʿa itself makes philosophical study obligatory for those capable of it: since truth is one, demonstration and revelation cannot truly disagree, and apparent conflicts call for interpretation.14, 18

Why it mattered

His line of commentaries on Aristotle — three tiers, from epitome to detailed exposition — was translated into Latin and Hebrew within decades and became the standard apparatus through which medieval Europe read Aristotle at all. “Averroism” convulsed the University of Paris in the 13th century; Aquinas wrote against him, Dante placed him honourably in Limbo. His medical summa al-Kulliyyāt (Latin Colliget) circulated alongside the Canon.3, 15

The honest summary is neither “Islam invented the scientific method” nor “theology strangled science,” but something more interesting: a civilisation in which the religious impulse to seek knowledge, the legal culture of disciplined reasoning, and massive state patronage together sustained the world’s most productive scientific tradition for over half a millennium — and in which the debates about reason’s reach were carried on at the highest level on both sides. Ahmad Dallal’s Yale lectures are the best short guide to that balance.16

IX · Legacy & Impact

What Europe learned to say in Arabic

The transmission was concrete: named translators, in named cities, rendering named books — many still bearing Arabic words.

The main gate was Toledo, reconquered in 1085 with its Arabic libraries intact. Through the 12th century a translation industry grew there — Gerard of Cremona alone is credited by his students with over seventy works, including Ptolemy’s Almagest (from Arabic, c. 1175), al-Khwārizmī’s algebra, Avicenna’s Canon, and al-Rāzī. Charles Burnett has shown how deliberately coherent this programme was — a curriculum being imported wholesale.15 The second gate was Sicily and southern Italy under Norman and Hohenstaufen rule, trilingual and curious; Leonardo of Pisa — Fibonacci — learned the “nine Indian figures” from Arabic arithmetic in Béjaïa and spread them in his Liber Abaci (1202).3, 17

The intellectual debts run deeper than vocabulary: the university medical curriculum was built on Avicenna and al-Rāzī for centuries;11 Kepler’s optics answers Alhazen;3 and the mathematical machinery of De revolutionibus — the Ṭūsī couple, the Ibn al-Shāṭir lunar model — was worked out in Maragheh and Damascus, as Swerdlow and Neugebauer’s technical study of Copernicus details.9, 19 Even our star maps talk in Arabic: Aldebaran, Altair, Deneb, Rigel, Vega, Betelgeuse.

alcohol الكحل
al-kuḥl, a fine powder; later, any distilled essence.
alembic الإنبيق
al-inbīq, the still-head of the distillation apparatus.
elixir الإكسير
al-iksīr, the transmuting agent of the alchemists.
zenith
from samt al-raʾs, “the direction of the head.”
nadir نظير
naẓīr, “the opposite point.”
azimuth السموت

cipher / zero صفر
ṣifr, “empty” — the Indian zero in Arabic dress.

X · Further Reading

The shelf

A curated ladder, from a first evening’s reading to the technical literature. Everything below is a real, findable book or paper.

Start here
  • Jim Al-Khalili, Pathfinders: The Golden Age of Arabic Science (Penguin, 2010). The best popular one-volume tour, by a physicist.
  • George Saliba, Islamic Science and the Making of the European Renaissance (MIT Press, 2007). Short, sharp, and myth-busting on both the origins and the “decline.”
  • Ahmad Dallal, Islam, Science, and the Challenge of History (Yale, 2010). The science–religion relationship, handled with precision.
General histories & the translation movement
  • Dimitri Gutas, Greek Thought, Arabic Culture (Routledge, 1998). The standard account of why the translation movement happened.
  • David C. Lindberg, The Beginnings of Western Science, 2nd ed. (Chicago, 2007). Places Islamic science within the long arc from Athens to 1450.
  • Roshdi Rashed (ed.), Encyclopedia of the History of Arabic Science, 3 vols. (Routledge, 1996). Discipline-by-discipline reference by specialists.
  • Encyclopaedia of Islam, 2nd & 3rd eds. (Brill). The scholarly reference for biographies and technical terms.
By discipline
  • Mathematics: J. L. Berggren, Episodes in the Mathematics of Medieval Islam, 2nd ed. (Springer, 2016). Works the actual mathematics, accessibly.
  • Astronomy: David A. King, In Synchrony with the Heavens, 2 vols. (Brill, 2004–05); E. S. Kennedy, “A Survey of Islamic Astronomical Tables” (1956).
  • Medicine: Peter E. Pormann & Emilie Savage-Smith, Medieval Islamic Medicine (Edinburgh, 2007). The standard modern survey.
  • Chemistry: Lawrence M. Principe, The Secrets of Alchemy (Chicago, 2013), chs. 2–3.
  • Philosophy: Frank Griffel, Al-Ghazālī’s Philosophical Theology (Oxford, 2009); Peter Adamson, Philosophy in the Islamic World (Oxford, 2016).
Primary texts in translation
  • A. I. Sabra (trans.), The Optics of Ibn al-Haytham, Books I–III: On Direct Vision (Warburg Institute, 1989).
  • Frederic Rosen (trans.), The Algebra of Mohammed ben Musa (1831; reprints available) — al-Khwārizmī’s founding text in English.
  • W. A. Greenhill (trans.), al-Rāzī, A Treatise on the Small-Pox and Measles (Sydenham Society, 1848).
  • Ibn Rushd, The Book of the Decisive Treatise, trans. Charles Butterworth (Brigham Young UP, 2001) — bilingual edition.
  • Al-Ghazālī, The Incoherence of the Philosophers, trans. Michael Marmura (Brigham Young UP, 2000) — bilingual edition.
  • F. Jamil Ragep, Naṣīr al-Dīn al-Ṭūsī’s Memoir on Astronomy, 2 vols. (Springer, 1993) — edition, translation, and commentary on the Ṭūsī couple.
Transmission to Europe
  • Charles Burnett, “The Coherence of the Arabic–Latin Translation Program in Toledo in the Twelfth Century,” Science in Context 14 (2001).
  • Nancy G. Siraisi, Avicenna in Renaissance Italy (Princeton, 1987).
  • N. M. Swerdlow & O. Neugebauer, Mathematical Astronomy in Copernicus’s De Revolutionibus (Springer, 1984). Technical, but the definitive treatment of the Maragheh–Copernicus connection.
  • Victor Roberts, “The Solar and Lunar Theory of Ibn ash-Shāṭir,” Isis 48 (1957). The paper that started the Copernicus comparison.
